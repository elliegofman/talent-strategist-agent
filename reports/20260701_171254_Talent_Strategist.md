## Strengths

- **Exceptional process-building credibility at pace.** Ellie didn't inherit clean infrastructure at Agora — she joined a fast-scaling, venture-backed startup and rebuilt CRM architecture, workflow design, onboarding, and forecasting from scratch while the company doubled ARR. That pattern of building under pressure, without a template, is precisely what Palantir's JD is describing when it asks for someone who rebuilds processes "from the ground up."

- **Data fluency that is genuinely rare for this talent pool.** SQL, Tableau, HubSpot, 25+ dashboards, weighted forecasting models, ~90% forecast accuracy — most recruiting candidates who come through for a Talent Strategist role cannot demonstrate this level of quantitative rigor with their own hands. Palantir explicitly wants a "critical and data-driven approach to managing candidate pipelines," and Ellie's toolkit maps directly to that requirement, even if the domain application hasn't been tested.

- **Stakeholder range is already proven.** She has navigated the C-suite (LP presentations to 110+ investors, executive strategy sessions) down to front-line individual contributors (SDRs, AEs) within the same operational context — that is the exact span Palantir describes when it says "embedding from the C-suite to Forward Deployed Engineering." Most candidates have range in one direction; she has evidence at both ends.

- **Mission-orientation and resilience are demonstrated, not asserted.** Leading an emergency humanitarian mission to Vienna for 500+ Ukrainian refugees, serving as NCAA team captain through personnel conflicts, and sustaining a decade-long commitment to terminal illness volunteer work are not resume filler here — they speak to the kind of person Palantir's JD is actually describing when it references "intensity, meaning, and mission." Combined with a 3.92 GPA and a Division III athletic career, the pattern of performing under pressure across multiple domains is consistent and credible.

## Risk Flags

- **Zero recruiting experience is a genuine structural gap, not a framing problem.** The JD says "does not need to be traditional Recruiting experience" — but it still requires demonstrated experience identifying and working with exceptional talent. Ellie has never sourced a candidate, evaluated a hire, managed an offer, or built a referral network in any context. At Palantir specifically, where the hiring bar is the entire product, this is not a gap to rationalize away quickly. The burden of proof in the interview is on her to articulate a credible and specific theory of how she transfers into this craft.

- **The Maccabee → Agora connection warrants a brief probe.** Maccabee Ventures is an investor in Agora, and Ellie worked at Maccabee immediately before joining Agora. This likely means she was recruited into Agora through a known relationship, which is fine — but it also means her transition was relationship-facilitated rather than competitively earned. Worth understanding whether she can generate her own opportunities and relationships from a cold start, which is essentially what sourcing requires.

- **All measurable impact is in a single domain at a single company.** Every quantified achievement — the 144% win rate increase, the 2x ARR growth, the ramp reduction — comes from one role at one company. Agora is a well-funded startup, but the context is narrow. It is unclear whether her process-building instincts generalize to Palantir's context (defense, government, forward deployment, technical roles) or whether they were highly optimized for a specific PropTech/FinTech sales motion. Her playbook may not transfer cleanly to recruiting exceptional engineers and mission-aligned operators.

- **No demonstrated experience with technical talent or defense/government missions.** Palantir's Talent Strategists are embedded with teams like Forward Deployed Engineering — they need to credibly evaluate technical candidates and earn the trust of technically rigorous hiring managers. Ellie's background is in sales operations and finance. There is no signal yet that she can develop the technical vocabulary, the judgment on technical fit, or the mission-context fluency that Palantir's hiring teams will demand from day one.

## Interview Probe Questions

**1. The cold-start sourcing question**
*"You have no existing recruiter relationships, no ATS history, and no prior recruiting brand. You've been assigned to fill three senior Forward Deployed Engineer roles in 60 days. Walk me through exactly how you'd build your sourcing strategy from scratch in week one."*

> **What a strong answer demonstrates:** Specific, creative tactics (not "I'd post on LinkedIn") — referral mapping, community infiltration, event-based outreach, leveraging internal Palantir networks. Shows she has thought through talent acquisition as a discipline, not just assumed her ops instincts will carry her. Weak answers will be generic or will lean entirely on tools without strategy.

---

**2. The process teardown question**
*"Tell me about a process you built that you later decided was wrong. What data or signals told you it was failing, how did you decide to tear it down, and what did you replace it with?"*

> **What a strong answer demonstrates:** Intellectual honesty and data-driven self-correction — the JD specifically calls out "embracing breaking these apart and evolving when the terrain shifts." Her onboarding program and CRM redesign are the obvious candidates here. A strong answer shows the iterative logic, not just the end state win.

---

**3. The stakeholder embedding question**
*"At Agora you supported a 40-person sales org. Imagine you're now embedded with a 12-person Forward Deployed Engineering team at Palantir, and the technical lead doesn't believe a recruiter can understand what they actually need in a hire. How do you earn real trust — not polite cooperation — within the first 30 days?"*

> **What a strong answer demonstrates:** A concrete, humble strategy for domain learning and relationship-building in an environment where she starts with low credibility. Should show she understands the difference between being liked and being trusted, and has a realistic theory of how non-technical operators earn respect from technical teams.

---

**4. The candidate quality calibration question**
*"Palantir has one of the most selective hiring bars in tech — many rounds, high rejection rates, candidates who've passed Google and McKinsey screens. How would you develop your own ability to identify 'exceptional' in a domain you've never hired for? What does your calibration process look like in the first 90 days?"*

> **What a strong answer demonstrates:** Self-awareness about her current knowledge gap combined with a disciplined learning plan — shadowing interviews, building rubrics collaboratively with hiring managers, studying rejected vs. accepted candidate patterns. Weak answers will overstate her current ability to assess talent quality.

---

**5. The competing demands under fire question**
*"At Agora, you were the operational lead for a 40-person org during a period of aggressive growth. Give me a specific example of a week where three things were genuinely on fire simultaneously. What did you triage, what did you let burn, and what was the cost of those decisions?"*

> **What a strong answer demonstrates:** Mature prioritization logic under real pressure, including an honest account of what she sacrificed and why — not a story where she heroically solved everything. Palantir's JD emphasizes "competing demands" and "intense" conditions repeatedly; this tests whether her experience of intensity is real or polished.
