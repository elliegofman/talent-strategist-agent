- **The sourcing and closing gap is the central risk.** This role's core deliverable is finding and landing candidates in one of the most contested talent markets in the country — competing against Anduril, Anthropic, and OpenAI for the same cleared FDEs and deployment strategists. Jordan's resume shows zero evidence of ever having owned a search, written a cold outreach, negotiated an offer, or moved a passive candidate to close. Supporting 40+ hires as a coordinator is not the same thing. If Jordan cannot demonstrate concrete sourcing ownership in the interview, this is a disqualifying gap, not a developmental one.

- **"People Operations" is a different job than "Talent Strategist."** At Ramp, Jordan was enabling recruiters — building their dashboards, coordinating their processes, onboarding their hires. Palantir's Talent Strategist is the recruiter. The risk is that Jordan has excellent supporting skills but has never been in the seat this role requires. Promoting a strong coordinator into a full-cycle recruiting role at Palantir — where every hire is high-leverage and the pace is unforgiving — carries real execution risk in the first 90 days.

- **No demonstrated mission fluency for defense or national security context.** Palantir is explicit that interest in defense and its mission is a requirement — not a preference. Jordan's background is entirely in fintech (Ramp) and commercial financial services consulting (Accenture). There is no signal — not a club, a project, a volunteer role, a public statement — that suggests engagement with national security, defense technology, or government software. For a company that runs philosophical interviews and screens hard on mission alignment, this silence is notable and worth probing directly.

- **Ambiguity tolerance is unproven at scale.** Palantir's JD calls for comfort with ambiguity, and the market brief is clear that the Talent Strategist role requires operating both offensively and defensively in a fast-moving, under-resourced environment. Jordan's two professional experiences (Accenture process engagements, Ramp people ops) were both structured roles with clear deliverables and manager oversight. Leadership in athletics and volunteer work is a proxy, but it is not the same as owning a recruiting function with no playbook in a mission-critical environment.

## Interview Probe Questions

**1. Walk me through a search you owned from first outreach to signed offer — specifically one where the candidate was not actively looking.**
*What a strong answer demonstrates:* Specific sourcing channels used (LinkedIn Recruiter, boolean strings, talent intelligence tools), volume of outreach, response rate, how they personalized messaging, how they navigated the candidate's hesitation, and what ultimately closed the offer. A weak answer describes helping coordinate a search someone else owned. This is the single most important question to ask and the one most likely to surface the gap.

**2. At Ramp, you supported recruiting for 40+ hires. Tell me about the one hire you feel most personally responsible for — and what would have happened if you hadn't been involved.**
*What a strong answer demonstrates:* Specific ownership and accountability rather than process support. A strong candidate will be able to point to a hire where their judgment, relationship, or persistence made the difference. A weak answer will describe process contributions ("I built the dashboard that helped the recruiter track the pipeline") without a clear line to candidate outcome.

**3. Palantir is recruiting against Anduril, Anthropic, and OpenAI for the same profiles — many of whom are getting inbounded weekly. How would you approach sourcing a Forward Deployed Engineer who has a 98% response rate from other recruiters and no public signal they're open to new roles?**
*What a strong answer demonstrates:* Creative sourcing strategy (conference networks, GitHub/technical publications, alumni maps, referral chains), differentiated outreach framing tied to mission, and an understanding of what makes Palantir's value proposition compelling to this profile versus its competitors. A weak answer is generic ("I'd personalize the message and reach out on LinkedIn").

**4. You built a pipeline efficiency dashboard at Ramp that improved recruiter output by 25%. If I gave you access to Palantir's Greenhouse data starting Monday, what would you look for in the first two weeks, and what metric would you use to tell me whether our current recruiting process has a sourcing problem, a conversion problem, or a closing problem?**
*What a strong answer demonstrates:* Genuine data fluency — the ability to diagnose funnel health using stage-by-stage conversion rates, time-in-stage analysis, source attribution, and offer acceptance rate breakdowns. This validates whether the Ramp dashboard work was Jordan's intellectual contribution or a tool someone else designed that Jordan maintained.

**5. Palantir's mission sits at the intersection of data, AI, and national security. Tell me something specific about that mission — a contract, a product, a use case, or a public debate — that you find genuinely interesting or complicated, and why.**
*What a strong answer demonstrates:* Authentic engagement with Palantir's work, not rehearsed talking points. A strong candidate can reference AIP, the Maven contract history, the TITAN Army deal, or the civil liberties tensions in Palantir's government work — and have a real opinion about it. A weak answer is a vague statement about "believing in the mission" with no specifics. This question directly tests the most visible gap in Jordan's profile.
