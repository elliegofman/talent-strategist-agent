## Risk Flags

- **"Supported" vs. "Owned" — the autonomy gap is unresolved:** The most important word on Jordan's resume is "supported." She supported 40+ hires; she supported hiring managers. Palantir is hiring a full-cycle recruiter with day-one ownership — not a coordinator who grows into independence. Until the interview clarifies whether she was sourcing candidates, managing hiring manager relationships solo, and closing offers, this resume describes a supporting role, not a lead role.

- **No demonstrated sourcing capability for technical or passive candidates:** There is zero evidence of outbound sourcing — no mention of LinkedIn Recruiter, Boolean search strategy, passive candidate outreach, or building pipelines from scratch. In a market where Palantir is competing against Anduril, Shield AI, and Databricks for the same engineers, sourcing passive talent is the core skill. This is the single largest functional gap.

- **Mission alignment to defense and national security is entirely absent:** Palantir's culture treats mission interest as a genuine hiring filter, not a formality. Jordan's stated interests (fintech operations, workforce development volunteering, Spanish language) reflect a civic but distinctly non-defense orientation. A candidate who cannot articulate a genuine point of view on why Palantir's defense and intelligence work matters will struggle to close mission-driven candidates — and may struggle to clear Palantir's own cultural bar.

- **Seniority ceiling concern:** Jordan's career is two roles deep — a consulting analyst position and a People Ops associate role. At 4–5 years total experience, she is at the floor of Palantir's target range for a role that requires partnering with senior leadership and operating with full autonomy. If Palantir's hiring bar skews toward 4–6 years of full-cycle ownership (as the market analysis suggests it does for top performers in this space), Jordan may be entering this process one or two years early.

## Interview Probe Questions

**1. Walk me through a role you owned end-to-end — from sourcing the first candidate to closing the offer. What was the hardest moment, and how did you handle it?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* She names a specific role, describes proactive outbound sourcing she personally executed (not just coordinating inbound), and articulates a moment of real friction — a competing offer, a hiring manager disagreement, a candidate who went cold — and how she resolved it independently. If her example is primarily about scheduling, coordination, or Greenhouse administration, that confirms the autonomy gap.

**2. You're sourcing a senior backend engineer for Palantir's AIP product team. They're currently at Stripe, not applying anywhere, and have already turned down Google. How do you get them to take a call?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* She demonstrates comfort with cold outreach strategy, can articulate a compelling mission-and-equity narrative specific to Palantir's moment (not generic), and shows she understands what makes a passive candidate at a prestigious company move. Generic answers about "personalized LinkedIn messages" are insufficient — look for specificity on narrative construction and competitive positioning.

**3. At Ramp, you built dashboards that improved recruiter efficiency by 25%. Tell me what was broken before you built them, what metrics you chose to surface, and what decision it enabled that wouldn't have happened otherwise.**
*What a strong answer looks like:* She can describe the specific pipeline problem (time-to-fill lag? stage conversion drops? sourcing channel imbalance?), name the metrics she chose and why, and point to a concrete downstream decision — a sourcing channel they cut, a hiring manager workflow they changed. Vague answers about "giving visibility" confirm she built a useful tool but hasn't internalized data-driven talent strategy at the decision-making level.

**4. Palantir works on surveillance infrastructure, military targeting systems, and immigration enforcement programs — work that generates real controversy. If a top candidate raises concerns about the ethics of the work, what do you say?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* She doesn't dodge, doesn't give a PR answer, and demonstrates she has actually thought about these tradeoffs independently — not just rehearsed a talking point. Strong candidates will acknowledge the genuine complexity, share their own perspective on why they find the mission defensible, and describe how they'd help a candidate reason through it honestly. A candidate who visibly hasn't engaged with this question before is a significant risk for a mission-critical recruiting role at Palantir.

**5. Tell me about a time a hiring manager was convinced a certain candidate profile would work, and you believed they were wrong. What did you do?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* She describes a specific disagreement — not a vague "I've had pushback" anecdote — and shows she used data, market intelligence, or structured reasoning to reframe the hiring manager's view. At Palantir, Talent Strategists are expected to influence, not just execute. A candidate who deferred to the hiring manager every time, or who has no real example of this dynamic, signals she hasn't yet operated at the strategic partnership level the role demands.
