## Risk flags

- **No verifiable full-cycle recruiting ownership.** The most critical gap: every data point on Jordan's resume is coordination, support, and program design. There is no evidence she has owned a requisition from intake to offer signature — no sourcing campaigns, no candidate pipelines she personally built, no offer negotiations she led. At Palantir, Talent Strategists own the full cycle autonomously. This is not a minor gap; it is the core job function.

- **Mission alignment is a complete unknown — and Palantir treats this as a hard filter.** Nothing in Jordan's background — her roles, education, volunteering, or stated interests — signals any engagement with defense technology, national security, or Palantir's specific work in Gotham, Foundry, or AIP. Palantir and its peer set (Anduril, Scale) filter for mission conviction from the first conversation. A candidate who cannot articulate *why this work matters* will not advance regardless of operational skill.

- **Seniority and scope may be understated for this role.** Palantir expects Talent Strategists to push back on C-suite hiring managers, shape role calibration, and operate with full autonomy. Jordan's most senior stakeholder engagement appears to be working with hiring managers to write job descriptions — a collaborative support function, not an advisory or challenging one. Whether she can hold her ground with a VP of Engineering or a Forward Deployed Engineering lead who wants to move too fast is genuinely untested.

- **No demonstrated outbound sourcing capability.** The 2025 recruiting market research is clear: sourced (outbound) candidates are 5x more likely to be hired, and Palantir's most critical hires — ML engineers, deployment specialists, enterprise sales — will not come from inbound applications. Jordan's resume shows zero evidence of passive candidate sourcing, Boolean search, cold outreach strategy, or network cultivation for hard-to-fill roles. This is a specific skill that must be validated, not assumed from coordination experience.

## Interview probe questions

**1. Walk me through the last time you identified and engaged a candidate who wasn't actively looking for a job. What was the role, how did you find them, and how did you get them to take your call?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* Specific sourcing methodology (LinkedIn Recruiter, GitHub, conference attendee lists, alumni networks), a genuine cold outreach message she crafted, and evidence she built a relationship over multiple touches before converting to a screen. A weak answer redirects to inbound management or coordination tasks — that's a red flag that confirms the gap.

**2. At Ramp, you supported recruiting for 40+ hires. Pick one that almost fell apart — either the candidate was going cold, a competing offer appeared, or the hiring manager changed the requirements late. What did you do, and what was the outcome?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* Jordan taking initiative beyond her coordination role — directly re-engaging a candidate, brokering a comp conversation, or pushing back on a hiring manager. If she can only describe "escalating to the recruiter" or "updating the dashboard," that confirms she was operating in a support track, not an ownership track.

**3. Palantir's work includes building data platforms for intelligence agencies and the U.S. military. Some people find that work uncomfortable. Tell me specifically why you want to work on that — and what you know about what Palantir actually does for government clients.**
*What a strong answer looks like:* A substantive, specific perspective on Palantir's mission — not generic "I believe in technology for good" language. Ideally she can name Gotham or the Army Enterprise Agreement, articulate the tension Palantir navigates, and make a personal case for why she's actively chosen this. A vague or deflective answer is disqualifying here.

**4. You built pipeline dashboards at Ramp that improved efficiency by 25%. If I gave you access to Palantir's ATS data for the last two quarters — time-to-fill, source mix, funnel conversion rates, offer acceptance rates — what would you look at first and what would you do with what you found?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* A structured diagnostic framework: identifying bottlenecks by stage, examining source-of-hire against close rates, flagging roles where screen-to-offer conversion dropped. This tests whether her data fluency is analytical (she interprets and acts) or operational (she builds reports for others to interpret). Palantir needs the former.

**5. Describe a situation where you disagreed with a hiring manager about what the right candidate profile looked like. How did you make your case, and what happened?**
*What a strong answer looks like:* A real example of Jordan advocating a position — not accommodating — with a senior person. Evidence that she used data or market context to reframe the conversation, and that she was willing to sit in the discomfort of pushback. This directly tests the stakeholder influence gap. A non-answer ("I always try to align with what they need") should be noted as a concern.
